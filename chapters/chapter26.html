Chapter 26: Capacitance and Dielectrics

1. Definition of Capacitance

Capacitance (\(C\)) is defined as the ratio of the charge magnitude (\(Q\)) to the potential difference (\(ΔV\)) between two conductors: \(C = Q/ΔV\). It is a measure of the ability to store charge, with units in farads (F). Capacitors are used in devices like radios, power supply filters, and electronic flashes.

2. Parallel Plate Capacitors

A parallel plate capacitor consists of two plates connected to a battery. The capacitance depends on the area of the plates (\(A\)) and the distance (\(d\)) between them: \(C = ε₀(A/d)\). The charge accumulates until the potential difference matches that of the battery.

3. Combinations of Capacitors

Capacitors can be combined in parallel or series. For parallel combinations, \(C_{eq} = C₁ + C₂ + ...\). For series combinations, the reciprocal of the equivalent capacitance is the sum of reciprocals: \(1/C_{eq} = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + ...\).

4. Energy Stored in a Capacitor

Capacitors store energy as electric potential energy, given by \(U = ½CΔV²\). The energy is related to the electric field between the plates and is also expressed as energy density (\(u_E = ½ε₀E²\)).

5. Capacitors with Dielectrics

Placing a dielectric material between capacitor plates increases capacitance by a factor of \(κ\), the dielectric constant: \(C = κC₀\). Dielectrics enhance energy storage and allow higher maximum voltages without discharge.
