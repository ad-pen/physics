Chapter 25: Electric Potential

1. Electric Potential and Potential Difference

Electric potential (\(V\)) is the potential energy per unit charge, independent of the charge itself. It is a scalar quantity and varies across an electric field. Potential difference (\(ΔV\)) relates to the work required to move a charge in the field: \(W = qΔV\). Units: volts (\(V = J/C\)).

2. Potential Difference in a Uniform Electric Field

In a uniform electric field, potential difference can be simplified: \(V_B - V_A = -E \cdot d\), where \(d\) is the displacement. Electric field lines point in the direction of decreasing potential.

3. Electric Potential Due to Point Charges

The potential at a distance \(r\) from a point charge is \(V = kq/r\). For multiple charges, the total potential is the algebraic sum of individual potentials.

4. Equipotential Surfaces

Equipotential surfaces consist of points with the same potential. They are always perpendicular to electric field lines. No work is required to move a charge along an equipotential surface.

5. Electric Potential Due to Conductors

The potential difference between any two points on the surface of a charged conductor in electrostatic equilibrium is zero. The entire surface is an equipotential surface, and the electric field is zero inside the conductor.
